                                                   Multi-Agent Debate has emerged as a promising framework for improving the reasoning
                                                   quality of large language models through iterative inter-agent communication. However,
                                                   broadcasting all agent messages at every round introduces noise and redundancy that
                                                   can degrade debate quality and waste computational resources. Current approaches rely
                                                   on uncertainty estimation to filter low-confidence responses before broadcasting, but this
                                                   approach is unreliable due to miscalibrated confidence scores and sensitivity to threshold
                                                   selection. To address this, we propose Diversity-Aware Retention (DAR), a lightweight
                                                   debate framework that, at each debate round, selects the subset of agent responses that
                                                   maximally disagree with each other and with the majority vote before broadcasting. Through
                                                   an explicit index-based retention mechanism, DAR preserves the original messages without
                                                   modification, ensuring that retained disagreements remain authentic. Experiments on
                                                   diverse reasoning and question answering benchmarks demonstrate that our selective
                                                   message propagation consistently improves debate performance, particularly as the number
                                                   of agents scales, where noise accumulation is most severe. Our results highlight that what
                                                   agents hear is as important as what agents say in multi-agent reasoning systems. Code is
                                                   publicly available at https://github.com/DA2I2-SLM/DAR.


                                         1     Introduction

                                         Large-Language-Model-based Multi-Agent Systems (LLM-based MAS) have recently attracted increasing
                                         attention thanks to their ability to leverage the power of LLMs in modeling and coordinating multiple
                                         agents, enabling complex problem solving and distributed decision-making (Tran et al., 2025; Yan et al.,
                                         2025). Within this paradigm, Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) has emerged as a main direction for improving the
                                         performance of LLM-based MAS. By enabling multiple language model agents to iteratively exchange and
                                         critique each other’s responses, MAD has demonstrated consistent gains over single agents across a range of
                                         reasoning and question answering benchmarks (Chan et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024).
                                         Despite its promise, MAD faces a fundamental scalability challenge: as the number of agents and debate
                                         rounds grows, each agent has to process an ever-expanding context consisting of all peer responses from the
                                         previous round. This broadcasting of all messages introduces two problems. First, redundant responses,
                                         where multiple agents converge on the same answer through similar reasoning paths, provide little additional
                                         signal while consuming significant token capacity. Second, noisy or low-quality generations can actively
                                         mislead other agents, propagating errors across rounds rather than correcting them. These effects reduce the
                                         practical benefits of scaling debate and raise questions about MAD (Choi et al., 2025), particularly regarding
                                         what information agents should be provided.
                                         A simpler solution for this problem is to restrict inter-agent communication by using a fixed sparse topology
                                         (Li et al., 2024; Choi et al., 2025). However, this method fails to account for the varying relevance of
                                         messages, as a static topology cannot distinguish between critical and redundant information, potentially
                                         discarding useful context or propagating noise. Later work has sought to address message quality through
                                         uncertainty-based filtering, discarding responses whose confidence scores fall below a chosen threshold
                                         before broadcasting (Lin & Hooi, 2025; Tian et al., 2026). While intuitive, this approach has significant
                                         limitations in practice: confidence estimates from LLMs are often poorly calibrated (Kadavath et al., 2022;

may not generalize across models or tasks. Critically, all of these methods ignore a key property of productive
debate: disagreement. A high-confidence response that merely echoes the majority view contributes far less
to collective reasoning than a lower-confidence response that suggests an alternative perspective.
We argue that capturing disagreement among agent responses is central to effective debate, as diverse
reasoning paths are more informative than redundant ones. To this end, we propose Diversity-Aware
Retention (DAR), a MAD framework that, at each debate round, selects the subset of agents’ high-quality
responses that maximally disagree with one another and with the majority vote (Figure 1). Concretely, DAR
augments the standard debate protocol with three lightweight components. First, each agent’s prompt
is augmented with an uncertainty score for every peer response, computed as the average negative log-
likelihood over the answer tokens, allowing agents to weight peer responses according to their reliability.
By introducing the uncertainty score into the prompt as additional information for the agent’s generation,
we avoid the challenge of tuning the confidence threshold. Second, DAR prepends the majority vote from
the previous round to each agent’s prompt, providing an explicit consensus anchor across debating rounds,
which will be utilized in the next step. Third and most importantly, we introduce a filter agent that receives
all peer responses and the prepended majority vote, then outputs a set of agent indices identifying the
most mutually disagreeing responses. The original messages at those indices are retrieved and broadcast
unchanged. This index-based selection guarantees that the retained disagreements are original, enabling
a controlled intervention in the debate process. Remarkably, our method is agnostic to the underlying
communication topology, supporting both fully connected and sparse structures. We conduct extensive
experiments on six diverse reasoning and question answering benchmarks across four LLM backbones
ranging from 1.5B to 8B. Our results demonstrate that DAR consistently improves debate performance,
particularly as the number of agents scales, while reducing the computational cost of message broadcasting.

2     Related Work

Multi-Agent Debate. MAD was first introduced by Du et al. (2024), improving response quality through
simulated debates among multiple LLM agents. Extensions include assigning diverse roles (Liang et al., 2024;
Chan et al., 2023), introducing mechanisms such as diversity pruning and misunderstanding refutation (Es-
tornell & Liu, 2024), and designing more effective communication protocols (Li et al., 2024; Pham et al.,
2024; Liu et al., 2023; 2024). Recent work has explored richer signals to improve debate dynamics, such as
incorporating explicit confidence expression to guide interactions and mitigate premature convergence (Lin
& Hooi, 2025). In parallel, ensemble-style multi-agent frameworks like Tool-Use Mixture (TUMIX) leverage
diverse tool-use strategies with iterative refinement across agents, emphasizing the role of diversity and
coordination in improving reasoning performance (Chen et al., 2026). Despite these advances, MAD is
sensitive to hyperparameters and does not always outperform strong single-agent reasoning (Smit et al.,
2024). Debates may converge prematurely, and simple majority voting can achieve similar results (Estornell &
Liu, 2024; Choi et al., 2025), suggesting that improvements often come from candidate generation rather than
debate itself. Recent work addresses this by filtering information between rounds, for example, subjective
masking uses an LLM agent to self-evaluate each response, while objective masking discards low-confidence re-
sponses (Tian et al., 2026). Another approach increase candidate diversity through more aggressive sampling,
albeit at higher computational cost (Zhu et al., 2026). In contrast, our method does not rely on per-sample
judgments or expensive sampling. The proposed filtering module retains responses that disagree with the
majority vote, while ensuring that the selected subset preserves diverse viewpoints. This enhances MAD
performance by preserving informative diversity in a simple and computationally efficient manner.

Uncertainty and Diversity in Multi-Agent LLM Systems. Uncertainty estimation has been widely studied
as a proxy for reliability in LLMs. Prior work measures uncertainty using token-level probabilities (Guerreiro
et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2026), verbalized confidence (Lin et al., 2022), or semantic
entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023). More recent approaches extend this to multi-step settings, such as propagating
uncertainty across reasoning steps to better capture cumulative errors in agent-based systems (Zhao et al.,
2025). In multi-agent debate, incorporating confidence signals has been shown to improve aggregation and
reduce premature convergence (Lin & Hooi, 2025). Orthogonally, diversity has been identified as a key
factor in improving reasoning performance. Prior work shows that gains in multi-agent systems often arise
from diverse candidate generation rather than interaction itself (Smit et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025). Methods
such as self-consistency, prompt diversification, and multi-prompt ensembles explicitly encourage multiple
reasoning paths via parallel prompting, leading to more robust aggregation (Wang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2025).
These observations suggest a close connection between uncertainty and diversity, where uncertainty can help
identify informative or complementary candidates. However, existing approaches typically rely on explicit
uncertainty calibration or increased sampling cost, motivating lightweight alternatives that leverage implicit
signals, such as disagreement, to preserve informative diversity without explicit uncertainty modeling.


3     Method

3.1   Preliminaries

Consider a debate with N (N ≥ 2) agents { a1 , . . . , a N } over R (R ≥ 1) rounds. Let gr,i denote the generation
of agent ai at round r. The debate state at each round consists of the previous-round responses Gr−1 =
{ gr−1,1 , . . . , gr−1,N }. This captures the standard multi-agent debate setting, where each agent conditions its
response on the collective outputs of its peers via shared context aggregation, which may be implemented
through concatenation or more structured strategies.

communication (Lin & Hooi, 2025). We compute uncertainty using the average negative log-likelihood

3.3   Majority Vote as Additional Context

Large language models are known to benefit from richer in-context information, as demonstrated in few-shot
and in-context learning settings (Brown et al., 2020; Min et al., 2022). Inspired by the observation of Choi et al.
(2025) that majority voting alone can match the performance of full debate, we introduce a soft integration of
the majority signal into the debate process. Formally, let ans( g) denote the final answer extracted from a
generation g. The majority-vote answer at round r −1, referred to as the last vote, is defined as:

i.e., the most frequent answer among the agents’ responses in round r −1. At round r, we incorporate this
signal by prepending the last vote vr−1 to the prompt of each agent, together with the responses from the
previous round Gr−1 (See Appendix A.3 for the prompt template).
Providing the last vote serves as an explicit consensus anchor, reducing the need to infer agreement pat-
terns, which can be challenging for smaller models (Touvron et al., 2023; Riviere et al., 2024). It guides the
next-round generation in two ways: (1) reinforcing consensus by encouraging verification against support-
ing evidence, and (2) highlighting disagreements to promote focused refinement. This improves context
utilization while preserving the original debate dynamics1 .

3.4   Improving MAD by Promoting Diversity

While MAD benefits from aggregating multiple candidate responses, not all generations are equally useful.
LLM outputs are known to be sensitive to prompt variations and often contain redundant or low-quality
content (Zhao et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022). As the number of agents grows, such noise accumulates and can
hinder the effectiveness of subsequent debate rounds. At the same time, prior work has shown that diversity
in prompts or reasoning paths can improve answer quality, as different prompts encourage the model to
explore alternative reasoning trajectories and reduce correlated errors (Wang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2025).
To formalize that, we model each response gr−1,i as a noisy estimator xi of the true answer. We consider a
simplified homogeneous setting where: (i) each estimator is unbiased with identical variance, Var( xi )=σ2 ,
meaning all responses are equally reliable on average; (ii) estimators are exchangeable, with a constant
pairwise correlation ρ, i.e., Cov( xi , x j )=ρσ2 for all i ̸= j, which means responses can be co-varying; and (iii)
ρ ∈ [0, 1] captures the average redundancy among responses induced by shared prompts, model biases, or
similar reasoning paths (higher ρ means responses are more alike). Under these assumptions, the following
proposition quantifies how redundancy limits the benefit of aggregating more responses.

benefit. Conversely, when responses are sufficiently decorrelated (ρ ≈ 0), the variance decreases optimally
as O(1/N ). This implies that the gain from increasing the number of agents is not determined by N alone,
but by the diversity of their responses. Therefore, selectively retaining diverse, disagreeing responses is not
just a computational convenience, but a theoretically grounded strategy for maximizing the effective number
of independent signals.
To this end, we introduce a lightweight filtering module F that selects a subset of informative responses
before each debate round (Figure 1). The module is implemented using another LLM, making it training-free
and compatible with existing MAD pipelines. In contrast to prior extensions that redesign agent roles or
modify communication protocols, our filtering module operates purely at the generation selection stage and
requires no topological changes or additional parameters of the MAD systems.
Specifically, the filter agent F receives the N responses from round r −1 and the last vote. Its objective is
to select a subset of agent indices (IDs) whose responses will be retained for the next round. Rather than
selecting responses based on correctness or confidence, F is prompted to retain maximally diverse (i.e.,
disagreeing) answers, thereby preserving informative disagreement while removing redundant generations.
Formally, let the agent IDs at debate round r be Ir = {1, . . . , N }, the filter module F receives Gr−1 and the
last vote vr−1 as input, and outputs a subset of agent IDs to retain:

Importantly, F outputs only agent IDs, ensuring that retained content remains unchanged and avoiding

Illustrative Case Study. As shown in Figure 1, each agent observes only previous-round responses from
others, where repeated majority answers are filtered to preserve informative diversity in DAR pipeline.
Consider three agents producing two answers, A and B, where A is the majority and B is the minority. Since
A is redundant, only B is retained to form the debate context, exposing agents with answer A to a novel
signal and encouraging revision. For the agent already predicting B, as no additional novelty is introduced,
we fall back to the full context ( A, A, B) to avoid losing useful information. This mechanism amplifies
informative minority opinions while suppressing redundancy, guiding agents toward the correct answer.
Importantly, majority responses are not discarded globally, ensuring performance is not degraded when the
majority is correct. See Section 4.3 for further analysis.

DAR Scales Effectively with Increasing Number of Agents. Figure 2 shows the performance of all methods
as the number of agents N increases across models (see Appendix A.5 for details). Majority Vote remains
a strong baseline, but its gains tend to diminish beyond N =4, with limited improvement compared to
multi-agent debate methods as N grows. In contrast, debate-based approaches benefit more from additional
agents, though their improvements are not always consistent. DAR consistently outperforms all baselines at
larger N (e.g., N =4, 8), where its advantage becomes clearly visible. These results indicate that effectively
leveraging additional agents requires controlling what information is propagated as noise accumulates with
larger N, reinforcing that what agents hear is as important as what agents say.


4.3    Ablation Studies and Model Analysis

To reduce computational costs, experiments are conducted on two representative datasets (Arithmetics,
Form.Log.) and models (Qwen2.5-1.5B, Qwen2.5-3B).
Effect Of DAR Components. Table 3 analyzes the contribution of each DAR component. Removing the
uncertainty score (Sec. 3.2) or soft vote (Sec. 3.3) causes slight drops, indicating they guide more effective
agent interactions. For comparison, we include a hard distinct-answer filter that discards responses whose
final answer is a duplicate. Replacing the LLM-based filter with a hard, distinct-answer filter yields larger,
consistent drops, causing genuine disagreements to be missed or equivalent answers to be treated as distinct.
In contrast, LLM-based filtering performs soft reasoning-aware selection, retaining responses with different
reasoning paths while discarding redundant or weak answers, thereby preserving informative diversity.
Similarly, naively using text-based filtering that asks the LLM to retain messages directly, rather than selecting
by index, significantly degrades performance due to formatting inconsistencies, leading to information loss
and unstable selection.

To highlight the importance of disagreement as a filtering criterion, we explore alternative criteria such
as retaining only certain or similar answers. All prompt templates for these modules are provided in
Appendix A.3. As shown in Table 3, these variants offer modest improvements over Society of Mind, yet
still fall short of DAR. Overall, DAR benefits from combining LLM-based filtering with additional prompt
contexts like uncertainty and vote, balancing diversity and reliability.
 DAR Boosts Diversity. We further observe that our retaining mechanism improves the diversity of the
 generated response, as illustrated in Appendix Figure 11. We measure diversity as the average pairwise
 embedding distance (1− cosine similarity) among retained responses across two debate rounds. We use
 all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) to compute the embedding. DAR selects responses that
 are maximally dissimilar from both the current majority vote and other candidates, encouraging broader
 reasoning trajectories. Across datasets, DAR consistently achieves the highest retained diversity, contributing
 to improved performance.
 Qualitative Example. Figure 3 illustrates a scenario where two of three agents initially produce the same
 incorrect answer (117) while one produces the correct answer (124). Standard MAD fails to overturn the
 incorrect majority, whereas DAR preserves diverse reasoning, enabling the correct minority to propagate
 and ultimately converge to the correct result. This demonstrates DAR’s ability to recover from an incorrect
 majority by leveraging informative disagreements.
Efficiency Analysis. As shown in Table 4, DAR consistently reduces the number of messages compared to the
standard debate across models and datasets, achieving up to a 32% reduction (Qwen2.5-3B on Arithmetics).
The gains become more pronounced as the number of agents increases (N =8). Latency remains largely
comparable, with a modest increase of 3–14% (avg. ∼9%), indicating that DAR improves communication
efficiency without significantly compromising response speed (Appendix Table 11).
Performance In Long Debates. DAR achieves strong gains in the early rounds and consistently outperforms
other MAD baselines as well as Majority Vote, remaining the best-performing method across longer debates
(Figure 4). Interestingly, most MAD methods improve mainly in the initial rounds and quickly saturate as R
increases, suggesting that extended debate yields diminishing returns. This aligns with prior findings that
multi-agent debates tend to converge rapidly, after which additional rounds provide limited benefit and

may even slightly degrade performance (Du et al., 2024; Smit et al., 2024). That said, thanks to its filtering
mechanism, DAR maintains reasonable performance across larger rounds, outperforming other methods.

5   Conclusion
We proposed Diversity-Aware Retention (DAR), a lightweight multi-agent debate framework that improves
reasoning by selectively propagating mutually disagreeing responses. By preserving informative diversity
via an index-based mechanism, DAR reduces noise and redundancy without relying on uncertain responses.
Experiments across benchmarks and models show consistent gains, especially as the number of agents
increases, highlighting the importance of controlling information flow in scalable multi-agent reasoning.

Ethics Statement and LLM Usages
This work presents a method for improving the reliability and efficiency of multi-agent debate systems built
on LLMs. We do not introduce new datasets, and our experiments are conducted solely on publicly available
benchmarks using open-source LLMs. To support reproducible research, we will publicly release our source
code.
LLM Usage. LLMs were used during the course of this research for assistance with writing and proofreading.
Besides, our experiments also use LLMs as the agents. All scientific content, experimental design, theoretical
analysis, and conclusions are the sole work of the authors. All LLM-assisted text has been reviewed, edited,
and verified by the authors.
Potential Risks. Multi-agent debate systems, like other LLM-based pipelines, may inherit and amplify biases
present in the underlying models. While our method improves response diversity and reduces redundancy,
it does not explicitly address fairness or bias mitigation, and care should be taken when deploying such
systems in high-stakes settings.

A.2   Diversity-Aware Retention Algorithm

We provide the pseudo-code for our method (DAR) in Algorithm 1.

Algorithm 1 Efficient MAD with Diversity-Aware Retention (DAR)
    Input: Number of agents N, number of rounds R, initial responses G0 = { g0,1 , . . . , g0,N }
    Output: Final answer v R
 1: Initialize retained set: S0 = G0
 2: for round r = 1 to R do                                   
 3:     Compute last vote: vr−1 = mode {ans( g)} g∈Sr−1                                ▷ break ties randomly
 4:     Compute uncertainty scores: ur−1,i = ANLL( gr−1,i ) ∀ gr−1,i ∈ Sr−1
 5:     Apply diversity-aware filter: Irret = F ( Gr−1 , vr−1 )
 6:     if Irret = ∅ then
 7:         Sr = Gr−1                                                                       ▷ fallback: keep all
 8:     else
 9:         Sr = { gr−1,i | i ∈ Irret }
10:     end if
11:     for each agent i = 1 . . . N do
12:         Form prompt using Sr , vr−1 , and {ur−1,j }
13:         Generate response gr,i
14:     end for
15:     Collect responses: Gr = { gr,1 , . . . , gr,N }
16: end for
17: Compute final vote: v R = mode {ans( g R,i )}iN=1
                                                        
18: return v R
