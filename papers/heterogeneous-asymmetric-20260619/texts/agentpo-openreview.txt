                                                A BSTRACT
             Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) offer a powerful paradigm for solving complex prob-
             lems through distributed reasoning and collaboration. However, their effective-
             ness is often hindered by the challenge of optimizing interactions among agents.
             To address this, we introduce AgentPO, a novel framework that directly opti-
             mizes agent collaboration. AgentPO employs reinforcement learning to train a
             specialized Collaborator agent, which refines its interaction policy to enhance
             overall system performance within a fixed multi-agent topology. We evaluated
             AgentPO on multiple mathematical reasoning tasks, where it consistently out-
             performed strong baselines. With Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct as the actor model,
             AgentPO achieves accuracy improvements of +1.8% and +7.2% over strong base-
             lines like Role Assignment and EvoAgent, respectively. When using the larger
             Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model, these gains increase to +5.6% and +11.3%. Cru-
             cially, AgentPO achieves these results with remarkable efficiency: it requires
             only 500 training samples and operates at just 7.8% of EvoAgent’s inference
             cost, highlighting its superior scalability and practicality. The code is available
             at: https://github.com/sunlin-ai/agentpo.


1        I NTRODUCTION
Drawing inspiration from human collaborative intelligence, Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) offers a
powerful paradigm for addressing challenges beyond the reach of individual agents (Shinn et al.,
2023; Qian et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). By enabling agents to collaborate and take on specialized
roles, MAS unlock new frontiers in artificial intelligence (Jimenez et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2025;
Guo et al., 2024). Yet, realizing this potential faces a central challenge: enabling agents to cooperate
effectively and robustly (Cemri et al., 2025). While significant progress has been made in developing
individual agent capabilities, such as reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023b; Hu & Clune,
2023), memory (Zhang et al., 2024b; Lewis et al., 2020), and tool use (Schick et al., 2023; Qu et al.,
2025), integrating these components into a cohesive system remains a formidable task.
Current approaches to MAS design broadly fall into two categories. The first relies on manual
orchestration of agent workflows, a labor-intensive process that requires deep domain expertise and
meticulous prompt engineering (Shinn et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). However,
because LLM-based agents are inherently sensitive to prompt variations, this approach introduces
cascading instability: minor performance fluctuations in one agent can easily propagate and amplify
instability across the entire system (Verma et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024a). The
second category employs automated methods to search for optimal interaction topologies (Hu et al.,
2025; Zhang et al., 2025; Zhuge et al., 2024). Yet, due to the combinatorial explosion of possible
topologies, such search strategies quickly become infeasible at scale and often fail to discover truly
effective collaborative structures (Gao et al., 2025).
To address these challenges, we propose a different viewpoint. Rather than manually designing
complex agent topologies or searching the vast and fragile space of interaction structures, we con-
tend that a more robust path lies in optimizing the collaborative dynamics within a well-designed
topology. This reframes the core research question from “What is the optimal agent topology?” to

“Given an effective topology, how can we train agents to cooperate more effectively to maximize
system performance?”
Building on this insight, we present AgentPO, an innovative framework that makes LLM agents
better cooperate. Figure 1 illustrates AgentPO further, it operates by creating a partnership be-
tween two agent types: a lightweight Collaborator and a powerful Actor. The Actor, which can
be a state-of-the-art model, focuses on task execution, while the Collaborator learns how to help
it succeed. Crucially, we only train the small Collaborator. Using a simple success-or-fail reward
signal, it learns to provide effective hints, critiques, or suggestions to its Actor partner. This ap-
proach boosts the team’s overall efficiency and adaptability without the heavy computational cost
of architectural search or fine-tuning powerful but ultra-large Actor models, offering a scalable and
resource-efficient path to building cooperative MAS.
We evaluate AgentPO on multiple challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks. Our experi-
ments demonstrate that directly optimizing collaborative dynamics significantly enhances perfor-
mance. For example, on the AMC23 benchmark, AgentPO achieves a +5.0% improvement in
Pass@1 accuracy under the Hint-Actor topology and a +7.5% improvement under the Critic-Actor
topology, substantially outperforming systems that rely on architectural search or standard Actor
fine-tuning. Our main contributions are:

       • We propose AgentPO, a novel framework that focuses on optimizing collaboration among
         agents through reinforcement learning.
       • We present empirical results on complex reasoning tasks, showing that AgentPO achieves
         significantly enhanced performance, validating our hypothesis that optimizing collabora-
         tive dynamics is an effective strategy for improving system-level capabilities.
       • We offer a practical and scalable methodology for enhancing multi-agent systems: by train-
         ing small, specialized Collaborator within a well-designed topology, AgentPO achieves
         superior performance at a significantly lower cost.


2     M ETHODOLOGY

2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION

We formalize the multi-agent collaborative problem-solving task as follows. Given a problem dis-
tribution D, where each instance is a tuple (q, y) comprising a problem description q and its ground-
truth solution y, the objective is to train a system of agents to collaboratively produce a solution ŷ
that maximizes the expected reward over this distribution:
                                  θ∗ = arg max E(q,y)∼D [R(ŷ, y)] .                               (1)
                                             θ

where R is the reward function.

2.2   T HE AGENT PO F RAMEWORK

AgentPO’s architecture is founded on a functional decoupling of collaboration and execution, im-
plemented through two distinct roles: Collaborator Agent and Actor Agent. The Collaborator, gov-
erned by a learnable policy πθ with parameters θ, is the target of optimization. In contrast, the Actor
operates with a fixed, high-performance policy πϕ using frozen parameters ϕ. Our objective is to
optimize θ to maximize the system’s joint performance.
For any given problem q, the interaction proceeds sequentially. First, the Collaborator Agent gener-
ates an auxiliary signal z conditioned on a problem-derived context:
                                          z ∼ πθ (· | cθ (q)).                                     (2)
The composition of this context cθ (q) is determined by the specific interaction topology (detailed in
Sec.2.3). This signal z is then used to form an enriched context for the Actor Agent, which in turn
produces the final solution ŷ:
                                           ŷ ∼ πϕ (· | q, z).                                     (3)

Finally, the solution ŷ is evaluated against the ground-truth y to produce a binary reward:
                                              R(ŷ, y) = I(ŷ = y).                                          (4)
where I(·) is the indicator function. This scalar reward provides the learning signal to update πθ
via our proposed objective (Sec.2.4). This decoupling enables AgentPO to optimize for specialized
collaborative skills within the interaction topology, enhancing system performance without altering
the foundational capabilities of the Actor.

2.3       AGENT T OPOLOGIES

The interaction protocol between the Collaborator and Actor is defined by agent topology. A topol-
ogy specifies two key components: (1) the input context cθ for the Collaborator’s policy πθ , and (2)
the mechanism for integrating the Collaborator’s output signal z into the actor’s context. We adopt
and evaluate two representative and widely used topologies that embody two distinct collaboration
paradigms: the feed-forward mode and the feedback mode, as is shown in Figure 1. Other topologies
can be similarly defined and analyzed following this framework.

AgentPO under Feed-forward Mode. In this configuration, the Collaborator acts as a Hint
Agent, providing proactive guidance. Conditioned solely on the problem q, it generates a hint
h ∼ πθ (· | q). This hint is then prepended to the problem query to form an augmented context
for the Actor Agent, which then produces the final solution y ∼ πϕ (· | [q; h]). The resulting re-
ward trains the Hint Agent to generate maximally effective guidance. This topology embodies a
feed-forward, proactive model of collaboration.


                                                        3
Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026




AgentPO under Feedback Mode. Here, the Collaborator assumes the role of a Critic Agent,
facilitating an iterative refinement loop. The sequence begins with the Actor generating an initial
draft solution, yinit ∼ πϕ (· | q). This draft, along with the problem q, forms the context for the Critic
to produce a critique, c ∼ πθ (· | [q; yinit ]). Finally, the Actor conditions on the complete history—the
problem, its initial attempt, and the critique, to generate a refined solution, yref ∼ πϕ (· | [q; yinit ; c]).
The reward, computed from yref , trains the Critic to provide feedback that most effectively steers
the refinement process toward a correct solution. This topology models a reflective, feedback-driven
form of collaboration.

2.4   M ODULE O PTIMIZATION VIA GRPO

outputs as a baseline, eliminating the need for a separate value function. For each input q, GRPO

3     E XPERIMENTS

3.1   S ETUP

Datasets. For training, we use the MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021), focusing on problems
from difficulty levels 3 to 5, following the same setup as prior work (Liu et al., 2025). For eval-
uation, we benchmark our method across five mathematical reasoning datasets: (1) AIME24: A
collection of 30 high-school olympiad-level problems from the 2024 American Invitational Mathe-
matics Examination (Li et al., 2024). (2) AMC: A set of 83 intermediate-difficulty problems from
the American Mathematics Competitions, primarily in multiple-choice format (Li et al., 2024). (3)
MATH500: A randomly sampled subset of 500 problems from the MATH dataset, spanning alge-
bra, geometry, and number theory (Hendrycks et al., 2021). (4) MinervaMath: A benchmark of
272 multi-step reasoning problems (Lewkowycz et al., 2022). (5) OlympiadBench: A challenging
suite of 675 high-difficulty mathematics problems (He et al., 2024).

Models and Baselines. Our experiments employ several models from the Llama and
Qwen families, including Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey
et al., 2024), Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024a),
Qwen2.5-Math-7B (Yang et al., 2024b) and Qwen-Plus (Yang et al., 2024a). Among these,
Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct are employed as the Collaborator,
while the remaining LLMs serve as the Actor. We compare AgentPO against several strong base-
lines: (1) CoT: Zero-shot Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022). (2) Self-Consistency:
CoT with self-consistency, which selects the most frequent answer from multiple reasoning paths
(Wang et al., 2023). (3) Self-Refine: An iterative method where agents critique and refine their own
outputs (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023). (4) Multi-Agent Debate: A framework in which
agents debate their answers and aggregate peer feedback (Du et al., 2024). (5) Step-back Abstrac-
tion: a prompting method that encourages agents to first reason about the core principles underlying

Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026




a task, enhancing overall reasoning effectiveness (Zheng et al., 2024). (6) Quality-Diversity: an ap-
proach that generates and ensembles diverse solution candidates to better explore the solution space
(Lu et al., 2025). (7) Role Assignment: a strategy that assigns distinct roles to foundation models to
promote specialized thinking and improve response quality (Xu et al., 2023). (8) EvoAgent:a self-
evolving agent that autonomously performs long-horizon tasks through self-planning, self-control,
and self-reflection (Feng et al., 2025).

Evaluation Metrics. Following established practice (Liu et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025), our pri-
mary metric is Pass@1 (Chen et al., 2021). Pass@k measures whether at least one of k indepen-
dently generated solutions is correct. We focus on the more strict Pass@1 setting, which evaluates
the accuracy of a single generated response and serves as a robust indicator of model reliability.

Implementation Details. We conduct reinforcement learning training using the verl framework
(Sheng et al., 2025). We set the clipping threshold to ε = 0.2, and during training, we sample 16
rollouts per prompt at a temperature of 1.0, with a maximum response length of 2048 tokens. The
global batch size is 16, with a per-GPU mini-batch size of 4 and a learning rate of 1 × 10−6 . For
inference, we use the vLLM library (Kwon et al., 2023), setting temperature to 0.0 and top-p to
1.0. To ensure rigorous evaluation on mathematical problems, we incorporate verification functions
from Math-Verify. All experiments are conducted on a cluster of 1 compute node, equipped with 4
NVIDIA A40 40GB GPUs. We re-implemented multiple baselines for comparison with AgentPO,
details are in Appendix C.

doubled from 6.7% to 16.7%, and on OlympiadBench, it increased from 16.1% to 28.9%. These
findings suggest that a more advanced Actor model provides a stronger foundation for system-level
optimization, enabling the discovery and utilization of effective collaborative strategies, and thereby
unlocking the latent reasoning capabilities of large language models.

3.3                                 A NALYSIS

Impact of Collaborator Model Selection. To evaluate the impact of the Collaborator model, we
use the Hint-Actor topology, varying the Hint model while keeping the Actor model fixed. As
shown in Figure 2, AgentPO demonstrates strong robustness, consistently improving performance
across different Hint models. Interestingly, we find that the smaller Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct achieves
the highest accuracy when paired with the Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct Actor, outperforming the larger
Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. Moreover, Qwen-series models generally yield better results, which we at-
tribute to complementary strengths. Specifically, Qwen generates hints with reasoning patterns dis-
tinct from those of Llama, providing diverse insights that help the Actor overcome inherent biases.
This underscores a key principle in multi-agent systems: effective collaboration depends not solely
on individual model capacity, but on the synergy of complementary capabilities.

Inference Efficiency and Practicality. Inference cost measured in token consumption is a key fac-
tor for real-world deployment. We evaluate the trade-off between accuracy and token usage across
methods. Results in Figure 4 show that AgentPO achieves top-tier performance at remarkably low
cost. With Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, AgentPO reaches 31.5% accuracy with just 1522 tokens on aver-
age, far below Self-Consistency and Multi-Agent Debate, which use 5–12× more tokens yet achieve
lower accuracy. AgentPO is as efficient as lightweight methods like CoT and Role Assignment, but
significantly outperforms them. AgentPO’s efficiency stems from its design: collaboration is of-
floaded to a small, optimized Collaborator, enabling the powerful Actor to generate the final answer
in a single, guided pass. By avoiding iterative or sampling-heavy processes, AgentPO breaks the tra-
ditional accuracy–cost trade-off, offering a scalable, cost effective solution for advanced reasoning
in practice.


4    R ELATED W ORK

Multi-Agent Systems. The design of LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems typically unfolds at two
levels: enhancing individual agent capabilities and orchestrating their interactions. At the agent
level, key advances include Chain-of-Thought and tree-based reasoning for planning (Wei et al.,
2022; Yao et al., 2023b;a), self-reflection for refinement (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023),
tool use (Qu et al., 2025; Schick et al., 2023; Nakano et al., 2021), and retrieval-augmented knowl-
edge access (Lewis et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024b). At the system level, coordination strategies
range from parallel exploration with aggregation (e.g., self-consistency (Madaan et al., 2023)) to se-
quential refinement and multi-agent debate, which enhances reasoning through argumentation (Chen
et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). While these efforts establish foundational com-
ponents and architectures, our work focuses on enabling effective cooperation within such systems.

Automated Design and Optimization of MAS. A parallel line of research focuses on automat-
ing the design of these complex systems. Optimization of multi-agent system has been explored
across various dimensions, including prompts (Khattab et al., 2024), tools (Zhou et al., 2024b), and
reasoning strategies (Shang et al., 2025). The most ambitious approaches focus on discovering the
optimal workflow or topology. For instance, ADAS and AFlow leverage advanced search algorithms
and LLM-based controllers to navigate the design space (Hu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), while
GPTSwarm uses policy gradients to optimize the connections between agentic nodes (Zhuge et al.,
2024). In contrast to these approaches, which primarily focus on searching the vast architectural
space, AgentPO introduces a complementary paradigm: collaborative optimization within a fixed


                                                     9
Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026




topology. Instead of discovering what the workflow should be, we directly train agent modules on
how to cooperate effectively, using system-level rewards to foster emergent teamwork.

5   C ONCLUSION
We present AgentPO, a framework that shifts multi-agent system design from manual or costly
architectural search to collaborative optimization. By fixing the system topology and training a
lightweight Collaborator via reinforcement learning, AgentPO optimizes inter-agent cooperation
end-to-end. Experiments on mathematical reasoning show consistent gains over strong baselines,
demonstrating that optimizing interaction dynamics within a simple, fixed structure is more effec-
tive and scalable than exploring complex architectures. AgentPO enables efficient enhancement
of powerful models through small, task-specific Collaborators. Future directions include integrat-
ing high-level architecture search and extending to dynamic, multi-agent systems with adaptive
roles. AgentPO advances the path toward robust, adaptive, and truly collaborative AI for real-world
problem-solving.

C    BASELINES


In this paper, we implement eight agent baselines for comparison with AgentPO (Section 3.2). In
CoT, The actor is prompted to think step by step before answering the question. In Self-Consistency,
We sample N=3 responses and aggregate them using a final decision agent. In Self-Refine, The
model performs up to three refinement iterations, with early stopping if the critic determines the
answer is correct. In Multi-Agent Debate, Agents are assigned distinct roles, such as Reading Com-
prehension Specialist, Logical Reasoning Strategist, and Multidisciplinary Knowledge Integrator,
and engage in a two-round debate. In Step-back Abstraction, A principle agent first identifies the
underlying concepts of the task, followed by a CoT agent that uses this knowledge to answer the
question. In Quality-Diversity, We run three iterations to generate diverse solutions based on previ-
ously proposed answers. In Role Assignment, A routing agent selects a role from a predefined set,
and the corresponding expert agent answers the question in that role. In EvoAgent, Over two evo-
lution rounds, one new agent is generated from each parent via crossover and mutation. Finally, all
solutions are aggregated by a CoT agent. These baselines represent diverse strategies for multi-agent
reasoning and collaboration, providing a comprehensive comparison with AgentPO.

E      P SEUDOCODE

In this section, we provide the pseudocode for the AgentPO algorithm to clarify its implementation
and workflow, as described in the main text.


                                                   17
Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026




Algorithm 1 AgentPO Algorithm
 1: Input: Collaborator policy πθ , fixed Worker policy πϕ , problem distribution D.
 2: Input: Collaboration Topology T ∈ {Hint-Actor, Critic-Actor}.
 3: Initialize: Collaborator parameters θ.
 4: for each training step do
 5:     Sample a problem (q, y ∗ ) from D.
 6:     if T is Hint-Actor then
 7:         Define Collaborator context: cθ ← q.
 8:         Generate hint (collaborative signal): z ∼ πθold (· | cθ ).
 9:         Worker generates final solution: yfinal ∼ πϕ (· | [q; z]).
10:     else if T is Critic-Actor then
11:         Worker generates initial solution: yinit ∼ πϕ (· | q).
12:         Define Collaborator context: cθ ← [q; yinit ].
13:         Generate critique (collaborative signal): z ∼ πθold (· | cθ ).
14:         Worker generates final solution: yfinal ∼ πϕ (· | [q; yinit ; z]).
15:     end if
16:     Compute reward: R ← I(yfinal = y ∗ ).
17:     Store trajectory (cθ , z, R) in an experience buffer B.
18:     Update policy πθ using the GRPO objective on data from B.
19: end for
20: Return: Optimized Collaborator πθ .
